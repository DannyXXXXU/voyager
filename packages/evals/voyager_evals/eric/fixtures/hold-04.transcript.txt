These audiences sit here from 7 in the morning all the way to 10 at night. But the people on stage are even more extreme. They perform during the day, tear down the stage at night, then travel overnight to the next location and the next day they perform again, sometimes working non-stop for 36 hours. And what's even more unbelievable is that there are no tickets, anyone can watch for free. So how do they survive? What really happens behind the stage? Over the next two days, we are going behind the scenes to find out. This is rural China. It is 9am, there is still one hour before the performance begins. But the area around the stage is already packed with people. There is no security, there are no tickets, and there is no limit on the crowd. For this performance, some people arrive 4 hours early, just to get a good spot. The performance begins, back in the 1930s, Charlie Chaplin visited China and watched traditional Chinese opera, what you are seeing today in terms of stage design, costumes, and props. Looks almost the same as what he saw nearly 100 years ago. The only major difference is the length of the performance. To match modern pacing and audience attention, Chinese opera today often cuts certain parts of the story and keeps a total running time between 90 and 120 minutes. Backstage, the actors who are about to go on stage are getting ready with their makeup. There is no professional makeup team here. Every performer has to do their own makeup. Traditional Chinese opera is often based on ancient stories, with themes centered on morality, loyalty, and love. The makeup of each performer reveals the character before a single word is spoken, showing whether the role is loyal or deceitful, emotional or cold. Because there are no doors on the toilets, people have to call out first to check if someone is inside before using them. Not far from the stage, in an abandoned courtyard, a man who works as both the cook and the bus driver is preparing lunch for more than 50 members of the troupe. There is a toilet and a simple shelter here, and even these basic conditions are considered relatively good. This is the deputy leader of the troupe. Because he has high blood pressure, he is no longer suited for performing on stage, so he is now responsible for logistics and daily support for the group. In China, opera troupes are mainly divided into state-owned groups and private ones. State-owned troupes are funded by the government. Their performers have stable income, and they have better costumes, props, and equipment. This troupe, however, is privately run and must support itself financially. In the past, it mainly relied on audience donations to survive. Now, in an era where almost everyone is creating content online, the troupe has also started live streaming to generate more income. This troupe has built a following of nearly 1 million on the Chinese version of TikTok, while live streaming brings them attention and online traffic. The troupe leader has also started to worry about its negative effects. When it comes to live stream content, there is a clear disagreement between the troupe leader and his wife. His wife believes that everything should be shown, not only the performances on stage, but also the struggles and daily life behind the scenes. The troupe leader, however, insists on focusing only on the performances, believing that the audience should see the show itself and nothing more. When I asked her about those years during the COVID-19 pandemic, this is what the troupe leader and his wife said. In this moment, the hardship and struggles of a private opera troupe are not only the reality, but all the difficulties they've faced are fully revealed. Worn out equipment, patched up costumes, even a drum with a hole is taped up and still in use. A mother walks on stage to perform while others backstage help take care of her baby. A sound technician with a physical disability still travels with the troupe from place to place. They came together because of their love for opera, and they treat the troupe as their home. Every effort they make leads to one simple goal, to perform well and to make the troupe At 10.30 pm, the performance ends, and everything you see on stage, including the lights, curtains, and equipment, has to be taken down and transported to another city to be set up again. On average, the troupe performs three shows a day and relocates to a new place every three days. Everything used in the performance, including costumes and props, is sorted and packed into different boxes. In total, there are more than 100 boxes of various sizes, and each one can weigh anywhere from dozens to over 100 kilograms, which is roughly between 100 and over 200 pounds. The people loading them are the same actors and musicians who were just performing on stage moments ago. All the props and equipment eventually fill up an entire truck, and what is left behind is a ground covered in trash, creating a striking and overwhelming scene. The next destination is about 100 kilometers away, which is roughly 60 miles, and about an hour later we arrive at the site. Since most performances take place in rural areas, the roads are narrow and it is not easy for large vehicles to pass through. These three vehicles are everything they have, one is filled with live stream equipment, one carries the stage costumes and props, and the last one transfers all the performers along with their belongings. Every time they arrive at a new destination, it feels like a game of chance. If they are lucky, they can stay in an abandoned house like this. If not, they have no choice but to set up tents and sleep outdoors. At each new location, the better living spaces are usually given to the female performers first, while the unloading and heavy lifting are handled by the male performers so the women can rest. This is an unspoken rule within the troupe. The sound technician, who has a physical disability in his legs, also works as the electrician. He does not stop because of his condition, but continues working on the front line, alongside everyone else. When morning comes and the crowd gathers again, they switch from loaders back to performers, putting on makeup and preparing for the show, followed by performance after performance. For the next three months, this cycle continues. They arrive at a place, perform for three days, and then move on to the next destination to do it all over again. This is what everyday life really looks like for a private opera troupe in China.
